### **Quick Reference: MI Diagnostic Criteria**

**1. Definitions:**
- Myocardial Infarction (MI) is diagnosed when there's evidence of myocardial necrosis **in a clinical setting consistent with acute myocardial ischemia**.

**2. Key Criteria:**
- **Elevated Cardiac Troponins (cTn):**  
  - Any detectable troponin above the 99th percentile upper reference limit (URL).  
  - A rise and/or fall in troponin levels is crucial for diagnosing acute MI.

- **Supporting Evidence (any one of the following):**  
  - **Clinical symptoms** of ischemia (e.g., chest pain).  
  - **New ischemic ECG changes** (ST-segment elevation/depression, new LBBB).  
  - **Imaging evidence** of new loss of viable myocardium or new wall motion abnormality.  
  - **Coronary artery intervention or autopsy** confirming ischemic necrosis.

**3. Role of Biomarkers (Troponin):**  
- **Diagnosis:**  
  - A **rise and/or fall** in troponin levels is essential.  
  - The **specific cutoff** represents levels exceeding the 99th percentile of a normal reference population.  
  - Usually, **at least one value** above this threshold with a subsequent change supports the diagnosis.

- **Interpreting Levels:**  
  - **Increase of >20-50%:** Suggests acute injury.  
  - **Small elevations** require clinical correlation.  
  - **Serial testing** (e.g., 3-6 hours apart) helps confirm dynamic change.

**4. Imaging (Wall Motion Abnormality):**  
- **Supporting evidence** in cases where troponin is equivocal or in diagnosis of MI with non-diagnostic ECG.  
- **New regional wall motion abnormality** observed via echocardiography, MRI, or ventriculography supports myocardial necrosis.

**5. Additional Notes:**  
- The diagnosis depends on **clinical context, biomarkers, and imaging** together.  
- The **timing** of troponin elevation relative to symptoms is crucial:  
  - **Elevated within 3-6 hours** of symptom onset.  
  - **Returns to baseline** over days in recovery phase.
