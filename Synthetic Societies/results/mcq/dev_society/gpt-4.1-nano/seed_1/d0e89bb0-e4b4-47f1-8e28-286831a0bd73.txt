### **Mechanism of Posterolateral Corner (PLC) Injury**

**Overview:**  
PLC injuries typically result from high-energy trauma involving a combination of forces that stress the posterolateral structures of the knee.

**Primary Injury Mechanism:**  
- **Varus Stress:** A force applied to the lateral side of the knee, pushing it inward, which stresses the lateral collateral ligament (LCL) and associated posterolateral structures.  
- **External Tibial Rotation:** Rotation of the tibia outward relative to the femur, which tensions the lateral and posterolateral soft tissue structures, including the popliteus tendon and lateral capsule.

**Common Injury Pattern:**  
- The **valgus and rotational forces** occur most often during motor vehicle accidents, sports injuries (like a collision or a twisting fall), or contact sports.  
- **Hypotheses:** The knee is forcibly placed in a **varus position** while the tibia is externally rotated—this mechanism leads to stretching, partial tearing, or complete rupture of the posterolateral structures such as the LCL, popliteus tendon, arcuate ligament complex, and posterolateral capsule.

**Additional contributing factors:**  
- Hyperextension combined with varus and external rotation can exacerbate the injury.  
- The **valgus and rotational forces** often lead to multi-ligamentous injuries involving the ACL and PCL as well.

---

### **Summary:**  
The injury occurs when a force causes the knee to collapse inward (varus), while the tibia is forcibly rotated outward (external rotation), stressing and tearing the structures at the posterolateral corner.
