**Option A: Supine with neck flexed**  
- The typical position involves the patient lying flat with the neck flexed forward.  
- Flexing the neck opens the nasal passages and helps align the pharynx and esophagus, facilitating the tube's passage.  
- This position may sometimes be used during the procedure in a supine patient.

**Option B: Sitting with neck flexed**  
- Sitting position with neck flexion could be beneficial, especially in awake, cooperative patients, to facilitate insertion.  
- This position provides better access and visibility.

**Option C: Supine with neck extended**  
- Extending the neck in a supine position often makes passage more difficult because it reduces the alignment of the nasal, pharyngeal, and esophageal axes.  
- This is generally not recommended for nasogastric tube insertion.

**Option D: Sitting with neck extended**  
- Sitting with neck extended may be helpful in some cases, but usually less preferred because it may cause patients to feel uncomfortable, and the neck extension broadens the oropharyngeal space but may cause more difficulty in swallowing or swallowing coordination.

**Summary of typical technique:**

- The most common and recommended position for NG tube insertion is **sitting or semi-recumbent with the neck flexed forward** (Option B).  
- This position maximizes alignment of the nasal cavities, pharynx, and esophagus.
